What the Deep Vault governs, who holds each line, and where the operational detail lives.

The two data classes

Class A — beta research data (personal): every participant from invitation to deletion — the VQ Assessment, the companion questionnaire (burnout-risk and psychological-safety items), the qualitative layer (free-text item, recorded debriefs, the optional mini-360), and the Greenhouse quarterly re-measures. Class B — pattern library data (engagement-derived): discovery-call recordings and transcripts (client staff are data subjects), client documents, and the abstracted, anonymised patterns retained by sector.

Roles & accountability · the RACI
  • Controller — PowerVox Ltd. Determines purposes and means for both classes. ⚑ confirm with reviewer
  • Consent / DP / IP owner — Scott. One architecture across both classes; external review the gate.
  • Pattern-library DP lead — Rachel. Class-B consent flow, pseudonymisation and privacy policy.
  • Research integrity — Hannah. Falsification criteria pre-set and held; the n=20 and n=60 gates; cave-month analysis.
  • External reviewer — to appoint. Independent legal check of the whole architecture before the first invitation.
  • Academic NED — to recruit. Arms-length oversight strengthening eventual published work.
Where the How lives

This playbook is the manual — the decisions and procedures, run play by play. How the work actually flows day to day is the operating model's How page, the Gather; the consent subsystem itself sits in the Burrows. Use those for the flow; use the plays here to run each governance move.

Set the basis · Play 1

Set the lawful basisbefore any capture

Decide, with the reviewer, the lawful basis for a data operation — and resolve whether the wellbeing items engage special-category rules.

Run this play when
  • A new data operation is being designed (the beta, a new instrument item, a class-B engagement).
  • The companion questionnaire's burnout-risk or wellbeing items change.
  • The reviewer is appointed and needs the basis put to them.
How to run it
  1. Test Article 9 first. The binding constraint is Article 9, not Article 6. On a cautious reading the burnout-risk and wellbeing items contain health data for at least some participants — so an Article 9 condition is needed on top of any Article 6 basis.
  2. Pick the Article 9 condition. The realistic two are explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)) or the research condition (Art. 9(2)(j) with DPA 2018 safeguards). The research condition fits a commercial category-building programme awkwardly.
  3. Set the Article 6 basis to pair with it (see routes).
  4. Hand the decision to the reviewer rather than pre-deciding it — the layered-consent design survives either way; only the Article 6 label moves.
Recommendation · the drafter's position

Consent-led. Explicit consent is the clean, proportionate route at a ~100-person scale, and informed consent is required for research ethics regardless — the academic partner will expect it. The freely-given test holds because participants are paying clients, not employees. The respectable alternative is legitimate interests (Art. 6(1)(f)) as the general basis with explicit consent solely as the Article 9 condition and for the layers where consent is unavoidable; this yields a more stable dataset. Held open for the reviewer.

Open decisions inside this play
  • The Article 6 label: consent-led vs legitimate-interests-led. ⚑ reviewer
  • If the items are not special category, legitimate interests becomes the strong primary route and consent shrinks to the ethics layer.

Set the basis · Play 2

Run a DPIA & map the processors

Screen each data operation for its risks, and map every controller and processor before tooling is locked in.

Run this play when
  • Any new data operation, instrument or cohort design begins.
  • A processor (assessment platform, storage, transcription) is about to be chosen.
  • The risk profile changes (a new sponsored cohort, a mini-360, a class-B engagement).
How to run it
  1. Confirm the controller analysis. PowerVox Ltd determines purposes and means for both classes. ⚑ confirm with reviewer
  2. Screen the DPIA triggers: systematic evaluation of individuals (scoring), potential special-category data (class A), vulnerable context (workplace power dynamics, sponsored cohorts).
  3. List the principal risks (carried into the per-play "where we are today"): re-identification in a small cohort, mini-360 candour about a named manager, recordings as high-sensitivity artefacts, pattern re-identification by sector insiders, commercial pressure on research framing.
  4. Hold tooling open. Leave the assessment platform unchosen until the reviewer has seen the processor map — deliberately. ⚙ platform, storage, transcription
Recommendation

Treat the DPIA as required, not optional, and complete the processor map as part of the same review so one legal check covers both products' foundations.

  • The controller/processor map once tooling is chosen — processors not yet selected. ⚑

Bring data in · Play 4

Design & operate layered consentclass A

Run the seven consent layers so each is granted and withdrawn on its own, and capture the baseline and qualitative layers only under the consent actually given.

Run this play when
  • A participant is invited to the beta.
  • A qualitative layer (free-text, recording, mini-360) is added to a cohort.
  • The consent texts need drafting or revising.
How to run it · one ask at a time
  1. Issue the participant information before any capture.
  2. Take consent layer by layer, each independently grantable and withdrawable: (a) participation & baseline · (b) research use of pseudonymised responses · (c) recording of debriefs · (d) the free-text reflective item · (e) the mini-360, with the respondent's own consent route · (f) anonymised case-study use · (g) onward sharing with a named academic partner.
  3. Capture the baseline (VQ Assessment + companion questionnaire) only under (a)/(b).
  4. Run each qualitative layer only where its specific layer is granted.
  5. Never bundle the opt-in moment with the commercial moment.
Recommendation

The layered design survives whichever Article 6 label the reviewer settles — only the label moves. Draft the seven consent texts on this architecture once the label is set, not before.

Bring data in · Play 5

Handle a sponsored cohort

Handle consent where an employer sponsors a cohort and staff may feel participation is expected — the one place the power-imbalance objection revives.

Run this play when
  • A sponsoring organisation funds a cohort.
  • Any cohort where participants are not self-funding.
How to run it
  1. Add explicit voluntariness language to the consent for that cohort.
  2. Add a no-detriment assurance — participation, or not, carries no consequence.
  3. Keep the sponsor out of individual results.
  4. Confirm the freely-given test is genuinely met for that cohort before capture.
Recommendation

For self-funding participants the power-imbalance objection is weak; for sponsored cohorts it is real. Do not rely on plain consent here — the voluntariness and no-detriment wording is the mitigation.

Keep it safe & use it · Play 8

Pseudonymise & secure

Split identifiers from responses within 30 days so the analysis never needs names — the core mitigation for both small-cohort re-identification and withdrawal.

Run this play when
  • Within 30 days of any class-A capture.
  • On class-B working artefacts wherever they are retained.
How to run it
  1. Pseudonymise within 30 days of capture; hold identifiers separately.
  2. Access-control the research store.
  3. Automate the n-counts so the gates announce themselves: n=20 hygiene · n=60 mid-beta · ~n=100 close.
Recommendation

Do this on schedule, not when convenient. Pseudonymisation is what makes the withdrawal carve-out (Play 12) defensible and the small-cohort risk manageable.

Keep it safe & use it · Play 10

Abstract a class-B pattern

Abstract a Loop engagement into organisation-unidentifiable patterns for the library — the anonymisation threshold is the whole game.

Run this play when
  • At each Loop engagement close, before anything enters the library.
How to run it
  1. Abstract to organisation-level form: sector, shape, lifecycle, governance archetypes, common gaps.
  2. Remove organisation identity to commercial-confidentiality standard as well as data-protection standard.
  3. Test against the ICO standard — not re-identifiable to an organisation by a sector insider.
  4. Only the abstracted pattern enters the library; raw transcripts and documents are handled per the contract (Play 13).
Recommendation

Once properly abstracted the pattern is outside personal-data scope — organisations have no UK GDPR rights. The threshold is everything, so have the reviewer confirm it before the second Loop client.

Handle a withdrawal

Process a participant's withdrawal cleanly, without destabilising the dataset — the known cost of consent as a basis, met by design.

Run this play when
  • A participant withdraws any consent layer.
How to run it
  1. Honour the withdrawal prospectively.
  2. Data already incorporated into analysis is retained in pseudonymised form — the standard research carve-out.
  3. Withdraw the specific layer, not necessarily all layers.
  4. Apply the carve-out wording confirmed with the reviewer.
Recommendation

Pseudonymisation within 30 days (Play 8) plus the prospective carve-out is the mitigation. Do not promise full erasure of already-analysed data — promise what the carve-out can honour.

Standing disciplines · Play 14

Contribution clause & IP

Carry the uniform pattern-contribution clause in every Loop contract, and hold the IP positions across both products.

Run this play when
  • Every Loop engagement contract.
  • Whenever IP is asserted externally (a claim, a publication, a trade-mark).
How to run it · the contribution clause

Every Loop contract carries one uniform clause: the client licenses PowerVox to abstract anonymised, organisation-unidentifiable patterns into the library; PowerVox warrants the anonymisation standard and that no client document or identifiable material enters the library; the client's own model remains the client's.

The IP positions
  • The instrument — VQ Assessment, Black Egg, companion questionnaire, scoring logic: PowerVox Ltd's.
  • Participant responses — stay theirs; PowerVox takes a research and case-study licence.
  • Derived data & abstracted patterns — PowerVox's, warranted anonymous.
  • TOM templates & the elicitation engine — PowerVox's (custody: Scott); clients licence outputs, never the engine.
Recommendation

One uniform clause across all engagements so confidentiality is consistent. Draft the clause with the reviewer; verify the trade-mark registrations before relying on the ™ marks.

Open decisions inside this play
  • Clause drafting. ⚑ reviewer
  • Trade-mark registration status — Voice Quotient™, the Voice Ladder™ — asserted, not yet evidenced. ⚑ verify
