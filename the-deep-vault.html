The IG playbook · launch-blocking · owner: Scott

The Deep Vault

PowerVox runs one data-protection regime across two operations: the VQ Assessment beta and the Loop pattern library. This is the operating manual for both — how data enters, is made safe, is used, and is let go, and the gate that holds until it has all been reviewed. A working draft for external legal review — the gate is not yet satisfied: the reviewer is unappointed and the foundation documents (privacy policy, terms, ROPA, DPIA) are still to be drafted. The Noticeboard holds the list.

Owain · the guardian

In Rachel's canon Owain is the red dragon, the guardian of the hearth and protector of Enfys, calm rather than fearsome. This is his work in the model. The Deep Vault is what he guards: the people's data, held safe, used only as promised, and let go when its time is done. Protection is not a feeling here, it is the controls this page specifies — a regime drafted, not yet passed, that has to hold before a single beta invitation goes out. The dragon gives the guardrails a guardian. ⚑ Proposed framing, drawn from Rachel's canon.

Data class A · personal

The beta

Every participant from invitation to deletion: the VQ Assessment, the companion questionnaire (burnout-risk and psychological-safety items), the qualitative layer (one free-text item, recorded debriefs, the optional mini-360), and the Greenhouse quarterly re-measures that make it longitudinal.

Data class B · engagement-derived

The pattern library

From Loop operating-model engagements: discovery-call recordings and transcripts (which hold client staff's personal data), the client's documents, and the abstracted, anonymised patterns retained by sector. Required before the second Loop client.

⚑ The launch gate · this whole architecture

No beta invitation issues, and no class-B abstraction begins, until this architecture has passed external legal review and the falsification criteria are locked. This page is the gate's artefact, not its satisfaction. The ten-year arc rests on the first dataset being honest and usable when the academic partner enters; one review buys both products their foundations.

The decision that sets everything · lawful basis

The binding constraint is Article 9, not Article 6. The burnout-risk and wellbeing items will, on a cautious reading, contain health data for at least some participants — which needs an Article 9 condition on top of any Article 6 basis. The drafter's position is consent-led; the respectable alternative is handed to the reviewer rather than pre-decided. The layered-consent design below survives under either route — only the Article 6 label moves.

Consent-led (the drafter's route)

Explicit consent (Art. 9(2)(a)) as the condition, and the clean Article 6 basis at this scale. The research condition (Art. 9(2)(j)) is built for institutional research and fits a commercial category-building programme awkwardly; defending that fit costs more than a ~100-person beta justifies. The freely-given test holds — participants are paying clients, not employees — except sponsored cohorts, which need explicit voluntariness and no-detriment language.

Legitimate-interests-led (the alternative)

Legitimate interests (Art. 6(1)(f)) as the general Article 6 basis, with explicit consent solely as the Article 9 condition and for the layers where consent is unavoidable anyway (recording, identifiable case studies, the mini-360). Permitted, common, and yields a more stable dataset on withdrawal. If the reviewer finds the items are not special category at all, this becomes the strong primary route.

Withdrawal instability is managed by design: pseudonymisation within 30 days and the standard research carve-out — withdrawal honoured prospectively, data already in analysis retained pseudonymised.

Consent architecture · class A · one ask at a time

Layered consent, each layer independently grantable and withdrawable. Consent unlocks one thing at a time — the opt-in moment is never bundled with the commercial moment. ⚑ Texts drafted on this architecture once the reviewer settles the Article 6 label.

a · Participation — programme and baseline assessment.
b · Research use — of pseudonymised responses.
c · Recording — debriefs; consent covers the recording specifically.
d · Free-text item — the optional reflective question.
e · Mini-360 — with the third-party respondent's own consent route.
f · Case studies — anonymised, specific consent.
g · Onward sharing — with a named academic partner under agreement.

Failure modes the design must beat · the DPIA risks
Re-identification in a small cohort

~100 people in narrow seniority bands. Mitigated by pseudonymisation within 30 days and access control.

Mini-360 candour about a named manager

Third-party respondents speaking about an identified leader. Own consent route and handling. ⚑

Recordings as high-sensitivity artefacts

In both classes. Audio deleted within 90 days; the transcript is the value, the recording the risk.

Pattern re-identification by a sector insider

Class B. Anonymisation tested against the ICO standard — not re-identifiable to an organisation by someone in the sector.

Commercial pressure on the research framing

Mitigated by Hannah's independent-critic mandate; thresholds set before the data arrives and not moved after.

Withdrawal destabilising the dataset

The known cost of consent as a basis. Met by pseudonymisation and the prospective withdrawal carve-out.
